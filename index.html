Pulsed tones and a point to hold your eyes on. A small instrument for practicing sustained focus — built to be honest about what it does and doesn't do.

01 — What this is

A metronome you listen into, not along with.

The core is an isochronic tone: a steady pitch switched rapidly on and off at a precise rate — say 15 times a second. Paired with a fixed point to stare at, it gives your attention a fast, regular target to lock onto.

The practice is simple. Fixate on the dot. Track the beats. Notice the exact moment you lose the count or your gaze drifts — then return. It's breath-counting meditation with a faster, externally-timed anchor.

Two other ways to make the beat are included. A binaural beat sends slightly different pitches to each ear (headphones only). A monaural beat mixes those two pitches in the air before they reach you.

Isochronic is the strongest, most clearly audible stimulus. Binaural is the subtlest and the one most of the research literature actually tested.

The honest caveat. The popular story — "match a tone to a brainwave and your brain follows" — overstates a real but weak effect. EEG shows the auditory cortex does track periodic sound (steady-state response), but robust, lasting changes in mood, focus, or "brainwave state" from these tones are not well established. Treat this as a focus-training and relaxation aid, not a brain-hacking device.

04 — How to run a session

A workable protocol.

The 4chan drill (focus training)

Method isochronic · smooth, band beta 15–20 Hz, carrier around 220–300 Hz. Fixate on the dot.

Wind-down

Method binaural, band alpha 10 Hz, glide toward theta 5 Hz.

Sensible limits

Keep volume comfortable. 10–20 minutes is plenty. Stop if you feel unease.

Do not use if you have epilepsy or a seizure history, while driving, or with a pacemaker without medical advice.
